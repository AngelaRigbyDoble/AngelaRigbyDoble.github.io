And the whole house was filled

He was back, and the whole house was full of it, roast meat, baked bread - our brother too, back from death, the stink only now completely washed away, lounged and laughed, eating and drinking by his side, glowing with life and clean sweat. My secret, costing everything I had, lay sealed in an alabaster bottle, hidden in my cloak. I snapped the neck and poured the unexpected perfume over his male-smelling head, his warm hands, smoothed his feet. Breath-taking, it rose through the whole house like lamplight or music, sweetness we will never savour again. He smiled and understood, wouldn’t let them criticise, gave my impulse-buy profound meaning. I knelt before him in tears, all of us and the feasting house to its roof filled with him. This poem won an American award for excellence in poetry

No man’s land

‘A cat is considered by law to be property. If you find a stray and keep it, you can be convicted of theft.’ (Notice in Ealing) The runaway cats you see on posters end up here. ‘I just need,’ they say, nosing round the door of my shed, ‘some space,’ slinking in, ‘to get my head back together.’ ‘And I’m “not required for breeding”,’ they add, curving around my leg. There is usually one curled up on the perished felt of the roof, another marking time under the apple tree by the old bath. And who knows how many in the bramble patch. ‘We want Millicent back!’ yells 020 8443 9705, stung by unaccustomed pain. ‘Sightings in the allotment area. Reward offered.’ I unscrew the thermos and sit in my doorway, dreaming of my reward. A new shed? Maybe a proper greenhouse? The late afternoon sun puts haloes on bean-rows and chrysanthemums, blackberries and weeds, half-blinding me. A Persian is watching from the next plot’s shadow, a ghost trailing soft white streamers, with small unhappy eyes. ‘I’m Millicent,’ she murmurs, and the sun moves, gilding her suddenly, and lighting up stained-glass windows in a number of tumble-down sheds. With a sigh I forget about wealth, and risk my freedom with a saucer of milk.

Painting class

The art of watercolour is to let the light shine through. Lay on transparent colours, wash after jewel-like wash, viridian, cobalt blue, rose madder - underneath I can still see disappointment, emptiness, fear of the future. Lay on the opaque colours, yellow ochre, cadmium red, cadmium yellow - underneath I cannot see the way ahead, the hand, the face of God. Everything around me looks like a painting - the cerulean sky, lemon yellow sun, sea, Prussian blue. But on my paper, layer on muddy layer; I have tried too hard. Scrub it off, start again! That is the art, to let the light shine through even the darkest shade, ultramarine, burnt umber, Paynes gray.
